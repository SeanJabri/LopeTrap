Campaign 1 - Quishing

What is Quishing?

Quishing, also known as QR phishing, is a term used to describe a type of phishing attack that leverages QR codes. It involves tricking individuals into scanning QR codes that lead them to malicious websites or prompt them to divulge sensitive information. While lesser known compared to phishing, quishing poses an equally significant threat.

Attack Description:

This phase of the social engineering campaign involved analyzing campus trends and creating posters designed to attract students, guiding them toward a QR code promising rewards or benefits. Once the user scanned the QR code, LopeTrap would track the scan, collecting minimal user information, and then redirect the user to a non-malicious website to avoid arousing suspicion.

Scope:

In Scope:

We are authorized to distribute benign QR codes strategically across campus, collecting the minimal amount of user information necessary.

Out of Scope:

Disrupting work or school operations is prohibited. Additionally, engaging in data theft or the exploitation of sensitive user data beyond what is necessary for tracking QR code scans is prohibited.

Attacks:

Attack 1 -Chick-fil-A

Description:

Recognizing Chick-fil-A's predominant popularity on campus, we tailored this poster to mimic a Chick-fil-A giveaway campaign. Incorporating Chick-fil-A's signature font, and utilizing a color palette of reds and cow prints, the design was meticulously crafted to mirror Chick-fil-A's branding. The QR code embedded within directed users to a designated link, intended to mirror Chick-fil-A's online presence, albeit leading to a strategically designed 404 error page.

Poster:

Metrics:

Metric Analysis:

This poster accumulated the most amount of scans of all other flyers throughout the period of time the campaign was active. Of the 218 total scans, a majority of them were recorded at the end of February and the beginning of March, when all or most of the posters were still in place. 191 of these scans were done on an iPhone, 26 on an Android, and 1 on a Windows 10 operating system. 86% of these scans were reported in Phoenix; the other 14% likely done on a device using a VPN.


Attack 2 - GCBC

Description:

Following an in-depth analysis of campus trends, it was observed that GCBC, the coffee shop at GCU, maintains a prominent presence with multiple locations across campus and significant popularity among students. Given the extensive visibility of GCBC posters and advertisements on campus, the deployment of additional materials would blend seamlessly without raising suspicion. The poster design strategy employed GCBC's distinctive purple and white color scheme, along with a font reminiscent of their branding, to maintain coherence with existing advertisements. Additionally, a coffee cup graphic was incorporated to suggest affiliation with GCBC. The QR code embedded in the posters directed scanners to a "Not Found" page on the GCBC website.

Poster:

Metrics:

Metric Analysis:

Of the 59 total scans, a majority of them were recorded at the end of February and the beginning of March, when all or most of the posters were still in place. There was then a dead period, when the posters had been taken down, before accumulating more scans after redeployment. 54 of these scans were done on iPhone devices, 2 of them on Android, 2 on a Windows 10 operating system, and 1 on a Mac OS X. 85% of these scans were reported in Phoenix; the other 15% likely done on a device using a VPN.


Attack 3 - TISU

Description:

The Threat Intelligence Support Unit (TISU) at GCU is a weekly training program for individuals working towards a career in security operations. At the end of this training program, participants must register for a final exam. Posters with alleged registration forms were strategically positioned near the classroom where the sessions took place. These posters were designed with fonts resembling those used in TISU advertisements commonly found within the Technology building. The QR code embedded in the posters led scanners to a meticulously crafted "TISU Registration Form," designed to resemble an authentic registration process, requesting minimal information such as email and name.

Poster/Form:

Metrics:

Metric Analysis:

Of the 17 scans, they primarily took place at the end of February/beginning of March and at the end of March, around the times of deployment and redeployment. 10 of these scans were done on iPhone devices, 4 of them on Android, 2 on a Windows 10 operating system, and 4 on a Linux operating system. 71% of these scans were reported in Phoenix; the other 29% likely done on a device using a VPN. 6 Individuals filled out the TISU registration form, all of which included their email addresses and full names (blocked out for privacy).

The initial poster featured a straightforward advertisement for a fictitious company named "Nozama," prominently displaying the phrase "We're Hiring" in large, attention-grabbing letters to pique curiosity and generate interest.

Attack 7 - Fortnite

Description:

Given our awareness of the ESports student body's interest in Fortnite, we developed a poster featuring the game's recognizable logo and theme. Positioned within the Technology Building, the poster offered an opportunity to win Fortnite currency, appealing to the targeted audience. The QR code redirected individuals to a Google form, requesting information such as email, Fortnite display name, and phone number, facilitating participation while adhering to data collection protocols.

Remediation:

Description:

Following a comprehensive analysis of our quishing poster campaign, we developed remediation posters aimed at raising awareness and educating the student body on social engineering tactics. These posters are designed to empower individuals with the knowledge and tools necessary to safeguard themselves and their data, irrespective of their prior cybersecurity knowledge. Our final designs feature a combination of warning messages, insights into potential impacts, and guidelines on safe QR code scanning.

In one of our poster designs, we included a secure QR code that, when scanned, redirects individuals to a Canva link reading, “Did you really just scan the QR code…. What did we just talk about?". This link directs users to a phishing quiz, providing an interactive opportunity to test their skills and enhance their understanding of cybersecurity concepts.

Our overall objective was to strategically position these posters alongside other QR code posters and our original quishing attack posters, maximizing visibility and ensuring that students encounter our educational materials before engaging with potentially malicious QR codes. This proactive approach aims to empower individuals to make informed decisions and mitigate the risks associated with social engineering attacks.
